Understanding DOMS: Is Soreness Necessary for Growth?

Michael Johnson

Certified Personal Trainer | Updated Feb 17, 2026

It's the classic joke: walking down the stairs like a penguin two days after leg day. We call it DOMSâ€”Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness. For many, it's a badge of honor, a sign that they "killed" their workout. But is pain really a proxy for progress?

In this guide, we break down what DOMS is, why it happens, and whether you should be chasing it or avoiding it.

1. What Causes DOMS?

Contrary to old myths, DOMS is NOT caused by lactic acid. Lactic acid clears from your system within an hour of exercise.

DOMS is caused by micro-trauma to the muscle fibers. When you lift weight, especially during the eccentric (lowering) phase, you create microscopic tears in the Z-lines of the muscle sarcomere. This damage triggers an inflammatory response. Fluid rushes to the area, causing swelling and sensitizing pain receptors.

This is why it is "Delayed." The inflammation peaks 24-72 hours post-workout.

2. Is Soreness a Sign of a Good Workout?

Short answer: No.

Soreness indicates two things:

  1. Novelty: You did a movement your body isn't used to.
  2. Damage: You created tissue disruption.

You can damage muscle without stimulating growth (e.g., running a marathon downhill). The advanced lifter rarely gets crippling soreness because their body has adapted (the "Repeated Bout Effect"). If you are getting sore every single workout, you might be recovering poorly or changing exercises too often.

3. How to Relieve DOMS

You can't "cure" it instantly, but you can speed up the process.

  • Active Recovery: Light movement (walking, cycling) pumps fresh blood into the muscle, flushing out metabolic waste.
  • Massage: Can reduce the sensation of pain.
  • Sleep: Where repair happens.
  • Protein: Provides the raw materials to fix the tears.

What DOESN'T work: Static stretching before the workout (can actually reduce strength) or intense icing (blunts the adaptation signal).

4. Should You Train While Sore?

It depends on the severity.

  • Mild Soreness: Yes. Once you warm up, the pain often fades.
  • Debilitating Pain (reduced range of motion): No. If you can't squat with proper form because your quads are too tight, rest another day or train upper body.
  • Sharp Pain: Stop. That is injury, not DOMS.

Conclusion

DOMS is a natural part of the lifting journey, especially in the beginning. But do not become addicted to the pain. Chase Progressive Overload (adding weight/reps), not soreness. A pain-free workout that sets a PR is far superior to a crippling workout that keeps you out of the gym for 4 days.

Disclaimer: This information is not medical advice. If pain persists for more than 7 days, see a doctor.
